TL;DR

Standard attention is slow and memory-heavy. ELSA fixes this by turning attention into a "Parallel Scan" problem[cite: 1, 7, 30]. It achieves O(log n) parallel depth and O(n) memory without losing any precision and requiring zero retraining[cite: 7, 32, 38]. Drop it into CLIP or SAM and get up to 3.5x speedup on FP32[cite: 39, 41, 122].

Abstract

Existing attention accelerators often trade exact softmax semantics or suffer from sequential depth that limits throughput[cite: 5, 17, 24]. We present ELSA, an algorithmic reformulation of softmax attention that preserves exact semantics in real arithmetic[cite: 6, 30]. By casting the online softmax update into a prefix scan over an associative monoid of triples $(m, S, W)$, ELSA achieves $O(n)$ extra memory and $O(\log n)$ parallel depth[cite: 7, 32, 122]. Implemented in Triton and CUDA C++, it improves FP32 inference latency over memory-efficient SDPA by 1.3–3.5x as a drop-in replacement with no retraining required[cite: 8, 10, 41].

Method: Parallel Monoid Scan

ELSA transforms the sequential online softmax recurrence into a fully parallelizable computation[cite: 120]. We prove that attention states $(m, S, W)$ form an associative monoid[cite: 38, 134].

We utilize a two-level scan architecture: intra-block Hillis-Steele and inter-block Blelloch[cite: 9, 142, 172]. This reduces the critical path to $O(\log n)$, which is particularly effective for FP32 where Tensor Core fusion is not applicable[cite: 173, 175].

Performance Benchmarks

Evaluations on NVIDIA A100 show that ELSA's throughput gains scale with sequence length[cite: 292, 294]. In FP16, ELSA achieves the lowest peak memory among exact kernels[cite: 42, 813].

Zero-Shot Deployment

ELSA serves as a drop-in replacement for pretrained foundation models[cite: 39, 52].

  • CLIP: Reduces latency by up to 224% and VRAM by 39.6% at high resolutions[cite: 411, 652].
  • SAM: Enables immediate acceleration without fine-tuning[cite: 39, 123].
  • 3D Vision (VGGT): Achieves up to 2.34x speedup for multi-view reconstruction[cite: 668, 681].
